A short, honest introduction

First question

What does a Data Scientist actually do?

Most people think it's about training models all day. The truth is less glamorous, more interesting, and far more valuable to the business.

Definition

Turning messy data into better decisions.

Data science sits at the intersection of statistics, code and domain expertise — extracting patterns from data so a business can act on them.

/ 01

Statistics

The grammar of uncertainty — knowing what a number actually means before believing it.

/ 02

Programming

Python, SQL and the engineering muscle to move data at scale and ship models reliably.

/ 03

Domain

Understanding the business well enough to ask the right question — not just answer it.

The toolbelt

Four skills do most of the work.

Everything else stacks on top of these. Master these four and you can have an honest conversation with any data team in the world.

/ 01

Python

Pandas, NumPy, scikit-learn — the working language of modern data science. Comfortable with structures, control flow and basic algorithms.

/ 02

SQL

Reading, joining and shaping data inside relational databases — where almost every business actually keeps its information.

/ 03

Statistics

Distributions, hypothesis testing and a healthy fear of overconfidence. The base layer beneath every model you'll ever touch.

/ 04

Machine Learning

Linear models, trees, clustering — knowing when to reach for which, and when a simple rule will outperform anything fancier.

Why it's a great profession

Compounding career, not a job.

Data science rewards curiosity. Every project teaches you a new corner of a business — and those lessons stack.

01

High leverage

One model can change how millions of decisions get made. Few jobs let an individual move a company's number that directly.

02

Paid like engineering

Strong technical comp, with senior salaries comparable to platform and backend roles in the same companies.

03

Domain mobility

The same toolkit travels — from banking to retail to healthcare to climate. You pick the field, you keep the craft.

04

Intellectually alive

You're paid to be curious. Each project is a small research problem, and the next one rarely looks like the last.

What good looks like

Think like the business. Build like an engineer.

The data scientists employers actually fight for share three habits — none of which are about knowing more algorithms.

/ 01

Translate the question

Turn a vague business goal into something measurable, then pick the smallest model that can answer it.

/ 02

Connect to revenue & cost

Tie every model output back to a number a CFO would care about. AUC is a means, not the deliverable.

/ 03

Communicate clearly

Present technical findings to non-technical stakeholders without losing the nuance — or their attention.

Getting started

There is no shortcut. There is a path.

The fastest people in this field aren't the ones with the most certificates — they're the ones who've shipped the most real things.

01

Get comfortable with the four basics

Python, SQL, statistics, ML fundamentals. Not glamorous, but everything stacks on top of these.

02

Work with messy, real data — not toy datasets

Kaggle is practice. Real banking, retail or operational data is where the actual skills are forged.

03

Build a portfolio that solves problems, not riddles

End-to-end projects with a clear business question, a dataset, a model, and a recommendation. Three of these beat ten certificates.

One thought to leave with

The point of data science isn't to build models.
It's to help a business make better decisions, faster, with fewer guesses.
